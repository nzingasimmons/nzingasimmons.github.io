My Project

Digitizing the Black Body: African American Contemporary Art and Digital Culture

Nzinga Simmons

Ph.D. Student in the Department of Art, Art History & Visual Culture
Duke University

Digital culture is increasingly ubiquitous. For black bodies in particular, the digital realm presents a conundrum. Simultaneously invisible and hyper visible, blackness on the web is consumed with virality in the form of popularized memes and GIFs, and more violently in images of police brutality amplified on social media. However, despite the proliferation of black bodies in cyberspace, those same bodies often go unconsidered in the construction of algorithmically driven digital technologies. In Safiya Noble's "Algorithms of Oppression" she writes,

"Although the rhetoric of the Information Age broadly seeks to minimize the hegemonic backdrop of the technological revolution, African Americans have embraced, modified, and contextualized technology into significantly different frameworks despite the relations of power expressed in the algorithms"

Employing Noble’s assertion about the radical and inventive ways that black people engage the digital as a touchstone, this dissertation examines African American contemporary artists working within the realm of New Media –– that is, making use of the internet, non-fungible tokens (NFTs), artificial intelligence AI), and other forms of digital technologies in their contemporary practice. This study centers its focus on African American contemporary artists who refuse the assumed neutrality of digital technologies, and instead conceptualize the digital realm as a context uniquely primed for the assertion of black futurity.

Artists of Interest

This dissertation focuses on a select number of African American contemporary artists whose praxis firmly opposes mythologies of the internet that allude to a technophobic blackness, reaffirmed by notions of the digital divide; and instead, produce work that questions and interrogates hegemonic digital cultures.

In their deployment of digital technologies, they demonstrate how the expansiveness of new media enables a discussion of race that acknowledges its permanence and complexity in the digital age. Further, this dissertation argues, that in the work of these artists, blackness itself is a deployed as a technology, creatively constructed in a digital context to challenge hegemonic power structures.

The objects engaged throughout this study lean into the technicity of race to upend or call attention to oppressive systems of representation and power that exist in a digital context. This study aims to question the normative whiteness that has subsumed net.art, internet art, and new media art discourses since their emergence at the turn of the millennia. Additionally, this study is reflective of my own positionality, as a black woman, born in the information age to a working-class family, and for whom the digital realm opened a multiplicity of worlds to explore and redefine.

Books of Interest

By focusing on African American contemporary artists employing digital technologies in their practice, this dissertation project contributes to a dearth of scholarship at the intersection of technology, contemporary art, and black cultural production.This study builds on the work of feminist scholars like Legacy Russell, whose critical work,"Glitch Feminism" (2020) leans into the liberatory potential of digital technologies to function as a mode of survival for marginalized communities.

Amid contemporary discourse about the internet’s pernicious influence (ie. mass surveillance, privacy infringement, data collection, algorithmic oppression etc.), This study opens new ways of conceptualizing how the internet can be a tool for marginalized communities to assert their agency and subjectivity in the twenty-first century. The following books have helped me think through ideas related to new media theory, art history, and black media studies. Additional text sources can be found under Notes.
